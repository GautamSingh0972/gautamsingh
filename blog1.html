Stuck in the Middle: Why Brazil Can't Escape the Middle-Income Trap

By Gautam Singh · Economics Blog · Based on Planet Money's reporting on Brazil

In 1980, Brazil looked unstoppable. Three decades of 7% annual growth had developed it from an agricultural backwater into an industrial powerhouse, something referred to as an "economic miracle" that appeared destined to carry it into the club of 1st world nations. Forty-five years later, Brazil is still waiting. It is the textbook example of the middle-income trap: the puzzle of why so many countries climb halfway up the development ladder and then simply stop.

This blog unveils the questions that Planet Money's podcast episode "The giant factory town that might be a giant mistake" left out, and what it could mean for countries like Brazil.

First, what really counts as "middle income"?

To understand the middle-income trap, we first need to define what a middle-income country is. The World Bank categorises countries by Gross National Income (GNI) per capita. Low-income countries sit at $1,135 or less per person per year. The middle-income band runs from $1,136 up to $13,934. The high-income finish line lies at $13,935.

The main problem is the large gap that lies between $1,136 and $13,934. Countries find it relatively straightforward to move from poor to middle-income by shifting workers from farms into factories. However, the transformation from middle to high requires solving a new puzzle: innovation, productivity, and institutions. Most countries never piece it together.

The blueprint that stopped working

In the 19th century, there was a reliable recipe for industrialisation, built on classical liberalism and the classical gold standard. It rested on three pillars:

  • Automatic price-specie flow — under the gold standard, if a country ran a trade deficit and gold flowed out, domestic prices and wages automatically fell, making exports more competitive and restoring balance.
  • Minimal social safety nets — governments didn't manage employment or provide welfare. Wages were highly flexible, and downturns were absorbed directly by the working class through unemployment and pay cuts.
  • Imperial expansion — empires like Britain's provided vast captive markets for manufactured goods and cheap raw materials, allowing unchecked industrial growth.

After the Second World War, every one of those pillars crumbled. The Great Depression and the war shifted public priorities towards social stability, and governments embraced Keynesian economics and started to take a more active approach to full employment and ending the era of freely falling wages. Strong trade unions meant workers could demand pay rises when inflation hit, breaking the old self-correcting mechanism and fuelling wage-price spirals. Decolonisation deprived industrial powers of their captured markets. And the Bretton Woods system, which replaced the gold standard, collapsed in the 1970s, proving inconsistent with nations pursuing their own inflation and employment targets.

The Zona Franca: a 60-year experiment in the Amazon

Nothing captures Brazil's dilemma better than the Zona Franca de Manaus. A free-trade zone planted deep in the Amazon rainforest, propped up by tax subsidies costing roughly $5–6 billion every year. After six decades, the factories still cannot survive without them.

Why haven't the factories learned to stand on their own?

Geography is the first culprit. Manaus sits thousands of kilometres from Brazil's main consumer markets, with no major highways connecting it to the wealthy south-east. Goods move by slow river navigation and coastal shipping, and every input has to be shipped in while every finished product is shipped out.

The deeper issue is structural. Factories in the zone mostly assemble imported Asian components rather than manufacture anything themselves, something called the "assembly trap." There is no local supply chain, and firms have optimised for tax credits rather than manufacturing innovation. Layer on the infamous "Custo Brasil", the country's infamous heavy bureaucracy and poor national infrastructure, and operating that deep in the jungle would bankrupt almost any unsubsidised business.

So is it worth the money?

The case against: an unsustainably high subsidy cost per job, products that can't compete outside protected markets, and protectionist tariffs that keep consumer prices artificially high for ordinary Brazilians.

The case for: the zone rescued a declining Amazonian rubber economy, sustains over 110,000 direct manufacturing jobs, funds around 40% of the state's revenue, and concentrating industry in one city appears to reduce deforestation in the surrounding Amazon.

The Lost Decade: How the 1980s broke Brazil

Brazil's best years were built atop a large amount of loans. The military government funded urbanisation and massive infrastructure using cheap, floating-rate foreign credit. When the US Federal Reserve raised interest rates in the late 1970s, just as global oil shocks hit, Brazil's borrowing costs surged overnight, causing its debt to become unsustainable.

What followed was a cycle of hyperinflation, fiscal collapse and chronic underinvestment now known as the Lost Decade:

  • Collapse of public investment — the state lost its capacity to drive development, abandoning critical infrastructure projects mid-construction.
  • The austerity trap — under IMF mandates, resources were diverted from public services to generate trade surpluses solely to pay interest on foreign debt.
  • Rampant hyperinflation — the government printed money to cover its deficits, sending inflation past 1,000% a year by the end of the decade.
  • Severe capital flight — uncertainty froze foreign investment and pushed domestic elites to shield their wealth offshore.
  • Industrial obsolescence — protectionist policies designed to save foreign currency trapped local factories in an outdated import-substitution model that couldn't compete globally.

A second chance? The China opening

However, the very force that closed the manufacturing door on Brazil may be reopening it. Average Chinese manufacturing wages have now climbed well above Brazil's. A weaker real makes Brazilian exports more competitive, and international friction is pushing multinationals to look for neutral regional hubs.

The most visible sign is a "green" reindustrialisation wave. Chinese auto giants like BYD are building major electric vehicle plants in Brazil, heavy foreign investment is flowing into wind and solar infrastructure, and Brazil's abundant clean power grid is a genuine magnet for low-carbon industry.

But old problems haven't changed. Their main exports remain raw commodities such as soybeans and iron ore; internal transport problems inflate delivery costs, and Brazil's complex tax code still drags on productivity.

The uncomfortable jobs question

Even if the new sectors boom, can they employ the millions who would once have worked in factories? No.

Advanced agriculture is highly capital-intensive. Modern mega-farms run on automation, drones and heavy machinery, employing very few workers per hectare. The jobs they do create, such as data analysts, agronomists, and drone pilots, don't match the skills of displaced factory workers, and they're located far from the cities where most unemployed Brazilians actually live.

Biodiversity-based innovation has the same problem on a smaller scale. Bioplastics and speciality cosmetics carry high margins but low volume as the roles are for scientists and specialised engineers, with none of the labour-heavy assembly lines of automotive or electronics. Most bio-economy projects are still in early testing phases. Meaning that these new opportunities can generate more revenue, but will not be able to reabsorb those who were working in medium- or low-skilled jobs in manufacturing plants and factories.

Is the government actually doing anything?

The Nova Indústria Brasil (NIB) programme injects R$300 billion through 2026, mandates cuts in industrial carbon emissions, provides substantial financing for local EV production, and offers low-interest credit through the state development bank, BNDES.

Alongside it, the National Bioeconomy Development Plan (PNDBio) supports the use of genetic resources in cosmetics. They have pledged funding for 6,000 community bio-businesses, expanded payments for forest preservation, and promoted indigenous herbal medicines in public healthcare. Sustainable agriculture receives support through subsidised loans, the RAIZ accelerator restoring ruined field lands, foreign partnerships such as Eco Invest Brasil mobilising private green capital, and regulation favouring bio-fertilisers over chemical alternatives.

The takeaway

Brazil's story is a warning about how development actually works. The ladder that today's rich countries climbed was pulled up behind them; the flexible wages, captive imperial markets and gold-standard discipline are gone, and no amount of subsidy can recreate them in the Amazon. Brazil's best shot now looks different from anything that came before; it comes from the green industry, clean energy and bio-innovation. Whether that can deliver not just growth but jobs is the question that will decide whether Brazil finally escapes the trap or spends another 45 years stuck in the middle.

Key Terms

Classical liberalism — a political philosophy centred on individual freedom, private property and limited government. States that people should run their lives and businesses with as little state interference as possible.

Classical gold standard — a monetary system (roughly 1870s–1914) in which countries tied their currencies to a fixed weight of physical gold, and paper money was fully convertible, creating highly predictable exchange rates for global trade.

Keynesian economics — the view that total spending (demand), not just market forces, drives output and jobs. Says that economies can get stuck with high unemployment, so governments should step in with spending or tax cuts. The multiplier effect means that initial spending triggers further rounds of spending, amplifying its total impact.

Economic downturn — a broad slowdown in economic activity, characterised by falling GDP, rising unemployment and similar indicators.

Middle-income trap — the pattern where countries grow quickly from low to middle income but stall before reaching high income, unable to make the leap to innovation-driven growth.
